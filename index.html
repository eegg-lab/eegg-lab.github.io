Our laboratory research interest focuses on how epigenetic and epi-transcriptomic changes act as interphase between the genome and the environment to facilitate the adaptation of the individual to changing conditions. We use various experimental approaches including Molecular Biology, Whole Genome Sequencing, Whole Genome Bisulfite Sequencing, methylation sensitive Genotyping By Sequencing, Reduced Representation Bisulfite Sequencing, RNA-seq, m6A RNA-seq, m5C RNA-seq and bioinformatics. We are currently venturing furtherinto the domain of bioinformatics by incorporating novel and advanced bioinformatics methods including, but not limited to, multi-omics integration, Systems Biology, Machine Learning etc.

Dr. Carlos López receives grant to study ‘memory’ in grapevines [Jun 2019]

show / hide content

LEXINGTON, Ky., (June 5, 2019) — Plants use memory at the molecular level to respond to stress in their environment. A University of Kentucky researcher, Carlos Rodríguez López, has received a $500,000 grant from the U.S. Department of Agriculture’s National Institute of Food and Agriculture, to explore epigenetic memory, continuing research he began while living in Australia. To survive and thrive, all living things need to cope with changes in their environment. Epigenetic memory is one of the molecular mechanisms that can change gene expression in response to a situation without affecting the actual sequence of genes. Exposed to the same stress more than once, plants handle it better, because they “remember.” López, assistant horticulture professor in the UK College of Agriculture, Food and Environment, is investigating how asexual reproduction using dormant cuttings, which is the way grapevines are commercially propagated, affects the plant’s epigenetic memory. “We want to understand what the mechanisms are that erase and maintain the epigenetic memory of a specific environment in plants. In particular, we will be looking at grapevines,” said López, who is the principal investigator on the project. “Because grapes are such an important crop, they are well understood, and there are also many different varieties. So, there is a fair amount of genetic viability that we can play with. That’s important.” López’s co-principal investigators are Dario Cantu, associate professor in viticulture and enology at the University of California, Davis; Sharyn Perry, UK professor in the Department of Plant and Soil Sciences, Robert Geneve, UK horticulture professor, and UK extension associate in horticulture, Jeffrey Wheeler. The team’s work could result in more vibrant Kentucky vineyards and, ultimately, better wines for the consumer. Lopez said the research findings will go beyond grapes and will provide important information that could be transferable to other perennial agricultural crops. Read full article

Lab member selected for presenting a talk in Evolution 2019 [May 2019]

show / hide content

Lakshay Anand has been selected to present a talk at Evolution 2019 conference to be held at Providence, Rhode Island, USA. He will present the research work that we are doing in association with a team at the University of Adelaide, Astralia.
TITLE : Identification of dicistronic tRNA-mRNA transcripts in vascular and non-vascular land plants
ABSTRACT: A dicistronic transcript is a single transcript containing two ORFs that are co-transcribed. Several such dicistronic transcripts, especially the one consisting of an mRNA and a tRNA (Figure 1), have been discovered in various plant species. Zhang et. al. (2016)(Zhang et al. 2016) used wild type Arabidopsis thaliana grafted onto a genetically modified rootstock expressing an artificial dicistronic tRNA:mRNA, to prove that such transcripts are transported from the transgenic tissue to the non-transgenic shoot, indicating that they have the potential to act as long-distance signaling molecules in plants. Moreover, using reporter genes they showed that the mRNA component of the dicistronic transcript is translated into a functional protein in the sink tissue. In-silico analysis performed by the University of Kentucky-University of Adelaide dicistronic tRNA:mRNA Research Group have shown the existence of these dicistronic transcripts in four flowering plant species including two dicots (Arabidopsis thaliana and Vitis Vinifera) and two monocots (Oryza sativa and Brachypodium distachyon) and their absence in a non-vascular species (Physcomitrella patens (mosses)) (Fei et al 2018). The analysis was performed using a customized bioinformatics pipeline and publicly available RNAseq data and validated in the laboratory for using RNA from V. vinifera. These preliminary results support the hypothesis that mobile dicistronic tRNA:mRNA are unique to vascular plants. To further testing this hypothesis, we will search for this transcripts in the remaining branches of the evolutionary tree of land plants. These will include, two non-vascular plant (Marchantia polymorpha and Marchantia inflexa (liverworts)), three ancient vascular plants (Selaginella moellendorffii (lycophytes), Azola filiculoides, Salvinia cucullate, and Pteridium aquilinum (Pteridophytes)). Based on the underlying assertion of the hypothesis, i.e. mobile dicistronic tRNA:mRNA transcripts evolved with plant vasculature, the predicted outcome of the project is described in Figure 2. Finally, comparative analysis will be done of the genomic sequence of intergenic regions between tRNAs and protein coding genes forming and non-forming dicistronic tRNA:mRNAs, to identify evolutionary conserved sequence motifs that could explain the underlaying mechanisms controlling the formation of dicistronic transcripts.

Common garden experiment reveals altered nutritional values and DNA methylation profiles in micropropagated three elite Ghanaian sweet potato genotypes [Published Apr2019, Plos ONE]

AUTHORS: Belinda Akomeah, Marian D. Quain, Sunita A Ramesh, Lakshay Anand, Carlos M. Rodríguez López
ABSTRACT: Micronutrient deficiency is the cause of multiple diseases in developing countries. Staple crop biofortification is an efficient means to combat such deficiencies in the diets of local consumers. Biofortified lines of sweet potato (Ipomoea batata L. Lam) with enhanced beta-carotene content have been developed in Ghana to alleviate Vitamin A Deficiency. These genotypes are propagated using meristem micropropagation to ensure the generation of virus-free propagules. In vitro culture exposes micropropagated plants to conditions that can lead to the accumulation of somaclonal variation with the potential to generate unwanted aberrant phenotypes. However, the effect of micropropagation induced somaclonal variation on the production of key nutrients by field-grown plants has not been previously studied. Here we assessed the extent of in vitro culture induced somaclonal variation, at a phenotypic, compositional and genetic/epigenetic level, by comparing field-maintained and micropropagated lines of three elite Ghanaian sweet potato genotypes grown in a common garden. Although micropropagated plants presented no observable morphological abnormalities compared to field maintained lines, they presented significantly lower levels of iron, total protein, zinc, and glucose. Methylation Sensitive Amplification Polymorphism analysis showed a high level of in vitro culture induced molecular variation in micropropagated plants. Epigenetic, rather than genetic variation, accounts for most of the observed molecular variability. Taken collectively, our results highlight the importance of ensuring the clonal fidelity of the micropropagated biofortified lines in order to reduce potential losses in the nutritional value prior to their commercial release.
